ECD-008Fire Clearance Certificate (Municipal Fire Department)For safety compliance – requires fire extinguishers, smoke detectors, evacuation plan and monthly fire drills – required for registration with DSD and for Public Liability Insurance claims. The inspection fee is typically R300 – R1,000, and you must keep proof of annual fire drill log. The certificate must be renewed annually. If you have a kitchen with gas stove, a gas certificate is also required. Fire extinguishers must be serviced annually and tagged – keep a logbook of monthly visual checks (visible gauge in green zone, no physical damage). The fire clearance certificate is non‑negotiable for ECD license and insurance. After fire clearance, you must display a "Fire Safety" sign at the entrance and keep a fire drill log for every month (date, time, number of children/staff, time taken to evacuate). Also keep a record of fire extinguisher inspection dates (monthly by staff, annually by certified company). For home‑based ECD (childminder), fire clearance is still required, but the requirements are simplified (one extinguisher in kitchen/living area, smoke detector, evacuation plan posted). The ECD facility must have two emergency exits if more than 10 children. The fire clearance certificate must be renewed every year, and the fire extinguishers must be serviced every 12 months. Keep a folder with all fire safety documents (certificate, service records, drill log) as inspectors will ask for it during annual DSD visits. If you are applying for DSD registration for the first time, fire clearance is one of the top five documents they require (along with health certificate, zoning certificate, police clearance for staff, and public liability insurance). Without fire clearance, DSD will not register your ECD. So get this done early – it can take 2–4 weeks depending on your municipality. If you are operating from a residential property, check with your local fire department whether you need a fire suppression system (sprinklers) – usually not unless you have more than 25 children. Most small ECDs (under 20 children) just need portable fire extinguishers and smoke detectors. Also ensure your electricity certificate (COC) is up to date if you have any electrical work (outlets, lights, stove) – the fire department may ask for it during inspection, especially if the building is old. The fire clearance certificate will include a checklist of requirements: fire extinguisher size (minimum 2kg ABC dry powder), smoke detector locations (hallways, kitchen, sleeping areas), emergency lighting (if used for after‑hours or if the building has no windows), exit signs (illuminated or reflective), and a fire evacuation plan posted in each room (including a map showing the escape route and assembly point). You must also keep a fire drill log showing that you conduct a fire drill at least once per month – record the date, time, how long it took to evacuate, and any issues. The log must be available during any fire inspection. Non‑compliance with fire safety can result in fines, closure, and loss of insurance coverage. So prioritize fire clearance early in your ECD registration process (along with health certificate and zoning certificate). The total cost for fire clearance + extinguisher purchase + annual service can be R1,500 – R3,000. It's worth it for safety and legal compliance. In some municipalities, fire clearance is a prerequisite for the DSD registration process; they won't even process your application without proof of fire clearance. So get your fire clearance certificate before you submit your DSD application. If you are already operating without fire clearance, stop and get it immediately – you are at high risk of being shut down by municipal inspectors who do random checks. Also note that fire clearance is required for the renewal of your ECD registration every year – the DSD will ask for an updated fire clearance certificate annually. So keep it current. For ECDs with outdoor play areas, make sure the fire escape route does not go into a fenced‑off area that could be locked; the gate must open easily from the inside (no padlocks) and children must be able to exit quickly. The fire department will inspect the entire premises including the playground and storage areas. If you have a gas stove, a gas certificate of compliance is also required (separate from fire clearance). This is issued by a registered gas installer and must be renewed annually. The gas certificate covers the installation (pipes, fittings, gas bottles) and safety of gas appliances (no leaks, proper ventilation). If you have gas heaters or gas water heaters, same requirement. The gas certificate is usually R500 – R1,000 per inspection. Keep it in the same compliance folder as your fire clearance. For ECDs with a kitchen (almost all), the fire clearance also includes a check of the kitchen hood/extractor fan – if it is greasy, they will fail you. So clean your kitchen hood regularly and keep a log. Also ensure that fire extinguishers are mounted on the wall, not sitting on the floor, and are easily accessible (not blocked by furniture). The extinguishers must be within reach (not too high for adults) and the pressure gauge must be in the green zone. The pin must be intact and the seal unbroken. Staff must be trained on how to use the extinguisher (PASS method: Pull pin, Aim at base of fire, Squeeze handle, Sweep side to side). Keep training records. The DSD inspector may ask for proof of fire extinguisher training during the registration visit. The training can be done by a fire safety company (cost R500 – R1,000 for a group session) or you can do internal training and document it. The fire department may also do spot checks annually – they will ask for the fire drill log, extinguisher service records, and evacuation plan. If any of these are missing, they can issue a non‑compliance notice and require you to fix within 14 days or face fines. So it's best to stay on top of fire safety. For ECDs that operate from a church or community hall, the fire clearance for the building is typically the responsibility of the building owner, but the DSD will still require a certificate for the premises where children are cared for. You should get a copy of the building's fire clearance from the landlord. If the building does not have fire clearance, you will need to arrange an inspection yourself. The fire department may require a full building compliance check if you are renting a commercial space. That could be more expensive and time‑consuming. So check with the landlord before signing a lease. Also note that fire clearance for an ECD may require a sprinkler system if the building is two stories or if you have more than 50 children. For most small ECDs (under 20 children), sprinklers are not required. But confirm with your local fire department – regulations vary by municipality. In Johannesburg, for example, ECDs with more than 15 children need a fire risk assessment by a registered fire engineer (cost R2,000 – R5,000). That assessment will determine if sprinklers or additional measures are needed. In Ekurhuleni, the rules are similar but may be less strict. Always check with your local fire department before assuming you are compliant. The best approach: request a pre‑inspection from the fire department (some municipalities offer this for free). They will walk through your facility and tell you what needs to be fixed before the formal inspection. That can save you time and money. Once you get the fire clearance certificate, make copies and file one in the ECD's compliance folder and keep another in the vehicle (if you have transport) in case of an emergency or inspection when you are away from the facility. Also upload a scanned copy to your cloud storage (Google Drive) so you can access it from anywhere. The certificate has an expiry date – set a calendar reminder 2 months before expiry to arrange renewal. Fire clearance renewal usually requires a follow‑up inspection (can be less detailed than the initial one but still required). Budget for the renewal inspection fee annually. The renewal may be faster if you have kept good records (fire drill logs, extinguisher service records) and have no changes to the building. The fire department will likely still need to visit to verify that extinguishers are still in place and that you haven't made any unsafe modifications (e.g., locked emergency exits, added partitions, blocked escape routes). So don't make any changes to your ECD layout without notifying the fire department first. If you add a new room or change the use of a room, you may need a new fire clearance. For example, if you convert a storage room into a classroom for more children, you need to update the fire clearance. Failure to do so can void your insurance and cause compliance issues. So plan any expansion carefully and involve the fire department early. Also, if you have a fenced outdoor area, ensure that the gate can be opened from the inside without a key – this is a common fire safety violation. The fence gate must have a quick‑release latch or a crash bar that can be pushed open from the inside. The fire department will check this. If you use a chain lock or padlock, they will fail you. So install a lever latch or a magnetic lock with a manual release button. Also ensure that the outdoor assembly point is clearly marked and that the path to it is free of obstacles (toys, furniture, etc.). During fire drills, children must be able to reach the assembly point within a few minutes. The assembly point should be away from the building and not near any hazards (power lines, gas storage, traffic). Keep a log of the assembly point and any changes. The fire clearance certificate will also require that you have an emergency lighting system if you operate after dark or in a windowless room. For most ECDs operating only during daylight hours, natural light may be sufficient, but you still need battery‑powered emergency lighting in hallways and in any room without windows. You can buy plug‑in emergency lights (R200 – R500 each) that automatically turn on when the power fails. Install them near exits and in the kitchen. Test them once a month and record the test. Keep a log. The fire inspector may ask for proof of testing. So document everything. Fire safety is not just a regulatory requirement – it protects the children in your care. Treat it seriously, budget for it, and keep all paperwork organized. You will need fire clearance for the DSD registration, for insurance, and for peace of mind. Don't cut corners. Also remember that your staff need to know the fire drill procedures. Conduct a fire drill at least once a month, at different times of day (during naptime, during free play, during meals). Record the drill date, time, duration, and any observations (e.g., children who were scared, staff who performed well). Use the drill log to identify training needs. Post the evacuation plan on the wall in each room, with a "YOU ARE HERE" mark. Practice with the children until they can evacuate quickly and quietly without panic. ECD-009Zoning Certificate (Residential / Business)Must allow ECD use – confirm with municipality; many ECDs operate from homes but need a zoning variance or home‑based business permit. If you are renting a commercial space, the lease must explicitly allow ECD operation. The zoning certificate is required by DSD as part of the registration application. Without it, you cannot get a health certificate or fire clearance. So this is one of the first documents to get. The cost is usually R500 – R2,000 depending on municipality. Processing time 2‑6 weeks. If your property is zoned residential, you may need to apply for a "home occupation" permit or "special consent" for an ECD. Check with your local town planning department. If you are operating illegally (without correct zoning), you could be shut down and face fines. So get this sorted early.
ECD-010Food Handling / Kitchen Licence (if providing meals)If you prepare any meals or snacks on the premises (which most ECDs do), you need a food handling certificate from the municipality. This requires a kitchen inspection (separate from health certificate). The inspection checks cleanliness, fridge temperature, food storage, pest control, staff hygiene, handwashing facilities, and food safety records (temperature logs, cleaning schedules). You must have at least one staff member with a basic food hygiene certificate (online courses available for R200 – R500). The cost of the food handling licence is R300 – R1,000. Renewal is annual. Without a food handling licence, you can still provide pre‑packaged snacks (e.g., store‑bought juice, crackers) but if you prepare any food (e.g., toast, porridge, cooked meals), you need the licence. Most funders and parents expect that you provide fresh meals, so you will likely need it. Keep a food safety log: daily fridge temperatures (below 4°C), freezer temperatures (below -18°C), cooking temperatures (above 75°C for meat), and a cleaning schedule. The DSD inspector will ask for these logs during the registration visit. Also keep a record of supplier deliveries (date, product, expiry date). Store chemicals and cleaning products away from food. Have a separate sink for handwashing and for washing utensils. Label all food containers with date and contents. Follow FIFO (first in, first out) for perishables. Keep a food waste log. All these are part of good practice and will be inspected. The food handling licence is also required for your COIDA claim if a child gets food poisoning – otherwise your insurance may deny coverage. So treat it seriously.
ECD-011B‑BBEE CertificateFor government funding / tenders (if you are an NPO or company seeking grants from corporates or government, a B‑BBEE certificate is often required. Even if you are a sole proprietor or small business, getting an affidavit (for exempted micro enterprise) is free and easy. It shows a commitment to transformation and can help in fund applications. Most ECDs are classified as EME (Exempted Micro Enterprise) if annual turnover is below R10 million, so you can get a sworn affidavit from a commissioner of oaths (cost R50 – R100) instead of a full certificate. This affidavit is valid for one year and must be renewed annually. If you are a non‑profit company (NPC) with a turnover above R1m, you may need a formal B‑BBEE certificate from an accredited agency (cost R2,000 – R5,000). But most small ECDs qualify for the affidavit. The DSD does not require B‑BBEE for registration, but funders and some government grants do. So get the affidavit if you plan to apply for funding. It takes a few hours to prepare and stamp at a police station or post office. It's worth it.
ECD-012NPO / PBO Registration (if non‑profit)If you want to apply for donor funding, tax exemption, or government subsidies (e.g., DSD subsidy for qualifying families), you should register as a Non‑Profit Organisation (NPO) with DSD and a Public Benefit Organisation (PBO) with SARS. NPO registration (cost free) requires a constitution, board of directors, and a resolution to register. It takes 2‑4 months. PBO registration (free) takes 3‑6 months but gives you Section 18A tax‑deductible donor receipts. Many funders require NPO registration, so start early. DSD may also require NPO registration if you want to apply for the Early Childhood Development subsidy (part of the government's ECD funding programme). The subsidy is means‑tested (parents' income) and not all ECDs qualify. But NPO status is still useful for credibility and fundraising. The documents needed for NPO registration include: founding statement (form NPO 1), constitution (must meet DSD requirements), list of office bearers (directors), and a resolution to register. If your ECD is already a registered company (Pty Ltd), you cannot register as an NPO; you would need to convert to an NPC or start a separate NPO entity. Many ECD owners operate as a sole proprietor (no CIPC registration) and then register as an NPO with DSD. That is allowed: you can have an NPO that does not have a separate legal personality (it will have a board but you remain the owner/manager). The DSD prefers that you have a constitution with a board of at least three people (unrelated to you). You can be the ex‑officio manager but not the chair of the board. So NPO registration requires a shift in governance. If you want to keep full control, you may prefer a private company (Pty Ltd) for profit. There is no subsidy for for‑profit ECDs, but you can still get funding from corporate social investment (CSI) programmes that do not require NPO status (some prefer NPC, but not all). So decide early which structure suits your goals. If you are aiming for government contracts, NPO is almost mandatory. If you are a private fee‑paying ECD with no subsidy, Pty Ltd is fine. The document roadmap includes both options, but you only need one. For simplicity, we list both with a note that you must choose. Most small home‑based ECDs are not registered as companies; they operate as informal businesses and only register with DSD. That is legal as long as you are registered with DSD and have all other compliance certificates. You do not need CIPC registration for a small ECD (unless you have employees, then you need UIF and COIDA registration anyway). But for clarity, we include both.

Note on CIPC registration: For a small home‑based ECD, you do not need to register with CIPC. You can simply register as a sole proprietor with SARS (using your ID number as taxpayer reference). However, if you have employees (e.g., a cook, assistant teacher), you must register with SARS for PAYE and UIF. You also need to register with COIDA (Workmen's Compensation) even if you are a sole proprietor, because COIDA covers employees, not the owner. You can register for COIDA online at the Compensation Fund website. The cost is a percentage of your annual payroll (minimum around R200 – R500 per year). COIDA registration is mandatory for any employer, including ECDs with even one employee. The certificate must be displayed on the premises. The DSD inspector may ask for it during registration. So don't skip it. Also, if you have a company (Pty Ltd), you must register with CIPC and pay annual returns. That adds cost and admin. For most small ECDs with fewer than 5 staff, staying as a sole proprietor or partnership is simpler and cheaper. But note that a sole proprietor has unlimited liability – if a child is injured and sues, your personal assets are at risk. That is why Public Liability Insurance is absolutely essential. It covers legal costs and damages up to your limit (recommended R2 million or more for ECDs). So you can operate as a sole proprietor as long as you have good insurance. The choice is yours. This document roadmap provides the documents for both structures. Focus on the ones that apply to you.

If you change fees, give parents 30 days written notice and get them to sign a fee addendum. The Consumer Protection Act (CPA) applies to ECDs as service providers, so your contract must be fair and transparent. Avoid hidden fees. Also include a privacy clause (POPIA) stating how you use child's information. This is especially important if you share photos on social media or with third parties (e.g., therapists, photographers). You need separate photo consent (see below). The enrollment agreement should be in plain language, printed in at least 10pt font, and signed by both parents (if applicable). Keep a copy in the child's file and give a copy to the parents. Also attach the child information form (next) as an appendix. This is the most important client document in your ECD. Make sure you have a signed copy before the child's first day. If parents refuse to sign, do not accept the child – you are at risk without a contract. The DSD may ask for a sample of your enrollment agreement during registration, but it's not a requirement. However, it's best practice and protects you.

The form must be updated every 6 months or whenever information changes. Get parents to sign and date the form. Also include a space for the parent's signature acknowledging that they have provided accurate information. This form is critical for medical emergencies. Keep it in the child's file and also have a quick‑access binder with emergency contacts in the office. For outings, take copies of emergency contacts with you. For children with asthma, keep a reliever inhaler in the classroom (labelled) and train staff on usage. The DSD inspector may check that you have emergency forms for each child and that they are accessible. This is also a requirement for the health certificate. So keep them organized. Use a secure filing cabinet or password‑protected computer. Update forms annually or when a parent reports a change. ECD-015Medical Consent Form (for emergency treatment)Authorises ECD staff to seek emergency medical treatment (including ambulance, hospital admission, surgery) if the parent cannot be reached. This is crucial because hospitals will not treat a minor without parental consent except in life‑threatening emergencies. Having this form can speed up care. The form should include: parent's signature, date, and witness signature. Also list any known allergies (as cross‑reference) and the parent's consent for the doctor to administer necessary treatment. Some ECDs also include consent for giving over‑the‑counter medication (e.g., paracetamol) with parental authorisation. That should be a separate line item with a checkbox. The medical consent form is separate from the standard enrollment contract; it's a specific authorization that many clinics and hospitals require. Keep a copy in the child's file and also a copy in a "medical consent binder" near the first aid kit. For outings, carry the medical consent forms with you. Update annually. This document is strongly recommended by the South African Paediatric Association. Without it, you may face delays in emergency care that could be life‑threatening. So make sure you have a signed copy before the child attends any outing or even a regular day. If you are unable to reach a parent, the hospital may still treat a child if the condition is clearly life‑threatening, but the form provides clear legal authority and protects you from liability. Also, the form should state that the ECD staff will make a reasonable effort to contact the parent before treatment, but if they cannot be reached within a reasonable time, staff are authorised to consent. This is a standard clause. The form can be combined with the child information form, but it's cleaner to have a separate single‑page document that parents sign once. Some ECDs include this clause in the enrollment agreement; that is acceptable but less explicit. I recommend a separate form. The DSD does not explicitly require this, but it's best practice and many funders (e.g., Lottery) will ask for it during due diligence. So have it.
ECD-016Permission Slip (outings, photos, sunscreen)For any off‑site activities (walks to the park, field trips, swimming) you need a specific permission slip for each outing, including date, destination, transport method, supervision ratio, and activities. For photos on social media or promotional materials, get a separate photo consent (specify how photos will be used – website, Facebook, brochure). For sunscreen application, some parents prefer natural brands; get consent. Also for application of insect repellent, nappy cream, etc. The permission slip should include a checkbox for each activity and the parent's signature. Keep a log of all permissions. For regular outings (e.g., weekly walk to the park), you can get a standing consent for the term, but still inform parents each time. The DSD inspector may check that you have permission for outings if there is an incident. This is also a requirement for your Public Liability Insurance – they may deny coverage if you took a child off the premises without parental consent. So don't overlook this. The photo consent should allow you to revoke consent at any time; also specify that no names will be used. This protects the child's privacy. In the era of POPIA, you must have explicit consent to process personal information (photos are considered personal). So get it in writing. Keep consent forms in the child's file. For group photos, if a parent has not consented, you must ensure that child is not identifiable (e.g., blurred or not included). This can be administratively challenging, so many ECDs ask for blanket consent to simplify. However, you must respect parents who opt out. Have a system to mark those children. The permission slip can be a separate document from the enrollment agreement, or you can include it as an addendum. Either way, get it signed before any activity.
ECD-017Fee Schedule / Payment PolicyClear schedule of fees (daily, weekly, monthly), due dates (e.g., by 1st of month), accepted payment methods (EFT, cash, card), late payment penalty (e.g., 10% plus interest), deposit requirements (e.g., two weeks' fees as deposit), refund policy (non‑refundable deposit), and consequences of non‑payment (suspension after 14 days, termination after 30 days). Also include any sibling discounts, government subsidy provisions (if applicable), and surcharges for early drop‑off or late pick‑up. The fee schedule must be provided to parents before they enroll, and any changes must be communicated with at least 30 days' notice in writing (signed acknowledgment). This is a Consumer Protection Act requirement. Keep a log of fee changes and parent acknowledgments. The payment policy should also state that the ECD is not liable for bank charges or EFT delays. Also include a policy on NSF cheques (bank charges to be borne by parent). The fee schedule is part of the enrollment agreement, but can be a separate attachment. It is essential for financial planning and for resolving disputes. The DSD does not regulate fees, but they may ask for a copy of the fee policy during a complaint investigation. So have it clear and fair. If you receive government subsidies, you must have a separate policy on how those funds are used and accounted for (they usually require that fees be reduced for qualifying families). Keep records of subsidy applications and approvals. The fee policy should be reviewed annually and updated as needed. All parents must sign the fee policy at enrollment and again whenever there is a change. Keep copies. Also include a clause that fees are payable even if the child is absent due to illness or holiday (unless you have a specific credit policy). This prevents disputes. Many ECDs have a "no refund for absence" policy. That's acceptable as long as it's disclosed upfront. The fee policy is your financial lifeline, so be thorough.
ECD-018Late Pick‑Up Policy & Fee AgreementSpecify the procedure and penalty for late pick‑up (e.g., after 5:30pm). Include a graduated fee: R50 for first 15 minutes, R100 for each additional 15 minutes (or similar). Also state that if a parent is consistently late, the ECD reserves the right to terminate the enrollment. Provide emergency contact numbers for the parent to call if they are delayed. Have a system for staff to stay with the child until the parent arrives. The policy must be signed by parents. It is not required by law but is highly recommended to avoid conflicts. Some municipalities require a late pick‑up policy for ECD registration? No, but it's good practice. The policy also protects staff from being unpaid for overtime. Ensure that your employment contracts with staff cover the possibility of late pick‑up (e.g., they will be paid overtime). The fee structure must be reasonable and not punitive (CPA requires fairness). A late fee of R50 for the first 15 minutes and then R10 per minute is common. You can also require that the parent pay the fee directly to the staff member who stays late, as compensation for their time. That can be a simpler approach: parent pays R100 to the staff member for staying an extra hour. Write that into the policy. The policy should also state that if the parent fails to collect the child and cannot be reached, the ECD will contact the emergency contacts, and if still no response, the ECD may call social services (Department of Social Development) as a last resort. This is a serious measure, but you need to have it in writing to protect yourself from allegations of child abandonment. The policy must be clear, signed, and reviewed with parents at enrollment. Keep a copy. The late pick‑up log: record each incident (date, time arrived, fee charged, paid). This log can be used as evidence if needed. Also, if you have a no‑tolerance policy, you can terminate enrollment after a certain number of late pick‑ups (e.g., three). That should be stated in the policy. Termination for cause must follow the termination clause in the enrollment agreement (e.g., 30 days' notice). So coordinate the two documents. The late pick‑up policy is a key operational document that can prevent burnout of staff and maintain good relations with parents. Don't overlook it.
ECD-019Sickness / Exclusion Policy (signed by parent)List of symptoms that require a child to stay home (fever >38°C, vomiting, diarrhoea, rash, conjunctivitis, etc.) and the requirement that the child be symptom‑free for 24 hours (without medication) before returning. Also state procedures if a child becomes ill during the day (isolation, parent contact, pickup within 1 hour). The policy should also cover when a child is excluded due to infectious diseases (e.g., chickenpox, measles, hand‑foot‑mouth). Reference the National Institute for Communicable Diseases (NICD) guidelines. Parents must sign that they have read and agree to comply. This is critical for infection control and for protecting all children. The DSD inspector will ask for a sickness policy as part of the health compliance. Display the policy in the entrance and in each classroom. Keep signed copies in each child's file. This policy also reduces disputes about fees when a child is excluded (you may still charge fees; state that clearly). Also include a clause that if a child is sent home due to illness, no refund is given for that day. That is standard. The policy should also address medication administration (only with written authorisation and in original containers). Have a separate medication authorisation form (see below). The sickness policy must be communicated to new parents before enrollment. Review and update annually based on NICD updates. It is also a requirement for your health certificate: the health inspector will check that you have a sickness policy and that you follow it. So have it signed and filed. This is non‑negotiable for ECDs. If you fail to exclude a sick child and an outbreak occurs, you could be liable for negligence. So enforce the policy strictly. Keep a log of children sent home (date, reason, parent notified). This log can be part of the incident report system. It also helps track illness patterns.
